How Much Tile Do I Need for a Backsplash? Complete Guide

July 24, 2026 · DIYCalcKit

A backsplash is one of the smallest tile jobs in the house, but it's also one of the most visible — every cut, every seam, and every shade mismatch sits right at eye level. Getting the quantity right the first time means one supply run instead of two, and buying from a single dye lot instead of a mismatched patch.

Step 1: Measure Width and Height

Measure the total width of counter you're tiling above, then the height from the countertop to the bottom of the upper cabinets. Standard kitchen backsplash height is 18 inches; a full-height backsplash running to the ceiling can be 24 to 36+ inches depending on your cabinet layout. Multiply width by height (in feet) for your gross square footage.

Step 2: Subtract Large Openings

Subtract large openings like a range hood or window from your gross area — but leave standard outlets alone. Your waste allowance already covers the small cuts needed around them, and subtracting every outlet individually just adds unnecessary math for a negligible difference in tile count.

Step 3: Add Waste for Your Layout Pattern

PatternWaste AllowanceWhy
Straight / Stacked10%Simplest cuts, mostly along the edges
Brick / Offset12%Row offsets create a few more partial pieces
Diagonal15%Every edge tile is an angled cut
Herringbone20%Most complex cuts and the most offcuts

Applying a straight-lay waste percentage to a diagonal or herringbone layout is one of the most common backsplash under-orders — the more intricate the pattern, the more of each tile ends up as an offcut.

Step 4: Convert Area to Tile Count

Find the area of a single tile in square feet (length × width in inches, divided by 144), then divide your total area (with waste already added) by that number and round up. A 3"x6" subway tile covers 0.125 sq ft, so a 16.5 sq ft backsplash needs about 132 tiles.

Grout: A Much Smaller Job Than It Looks

Backsplash grout needs are small compared to flooring — most standard kitchen backsplashes use well under one full bag. For an exact bag count based on your specific tile size and joint width, use a dedicated grout calculator rather than eyeballing it from the backsplash area alone.

Pro tip: Buy all your tile in one order from the same dye lot. Reordering later — even the "same" tile — often comes from a different production batch with a slightly different shade, which is very visible on a backsplash at eye level.

Common Backsplash Mistakes

  • Underestimating pattern waste. Straight-lay waste percentages don't apply to diagonal or herringbone layouts.
  • Forgetting the range hood or window cutout. Not subtracting a large opening means over-ordering tile for space you'll never fill.
  • Buying tile from multiple dye lots. A reorder mid-project can come from a different batch with a visibly different shade.
  • Ignoring outlet position when planning the layout. Grout lines that land right at outlet edges look unplanned — dry-lay the first row before committing to spacing.
  • Skipping edge trim. An exposed tile edge without bullnose or metal trim chips easily and looks unfinished at the end of a run.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much tile do I need for a kitchen backsplash?

A standard kitchen backsplash (18 inches high, 10 feet of counter) needs about 15 sq ft of tile before waste. Add 10% for a straight layout, 12% for brick/offset, 15% for diagonal, or 20% for herringbone to get your purchase total.

What height should I measure for a backsplash?

Standard kitchen backsplash height is 18 inches, measured from the countertop to the bottom of the upper cabinets. A full-height backsplash that runs to the ceiling can be 24-36+ inches depending on your cabinet layout.

Do I subtract the range hood area from my backsplash tile order?

Yes — subtract large openings like a range hood or window from your gross wall area, but don't subtract small openings like standard outlets, since the waste factor already covers cuts around them.

How much extra tile should I order for waste?

10% for a straight/stacked layout, 12% for brick or offset patterns, 15% for a diagonal layout, and 20% for herringbone or other complex patterns — more intricate layouts create more cut pieces and more waste.
