How Many Christmas Lights Do I Need? Roofline, Tree & Window Guide

July 21, 2026 · DIYCalcKit

Christmas light quantity comes down to one calculation repeated for each area you're decorating: measure the linear feet, then convert that to strands and bulbs based on your light type's spacing. Get the roofline, windows, doors, and any trees right, and the shopping list writes itself.

Step 1: Measure the Roofline

Measure along the eaves — the horizontal edges where the roof overhangs the wall — not the sloped gable ends. Walk the perimeter with a tape measure or measuring wheel from the ground, or trace it in Google Maps satellite view using the built-in measure-distance tool if you'd rather skip the ladder for the planning stage.

Add about 10% to your raw measurement. Corners and peaks eat up extra length as bulbs bunch up going around the turn, and you'll want a little slack for clips and plug-in points.

Step 2: Add Windows, Doors, and Trim

FeatureTypical Footage
Standard window outline~18 ft

These numbers assume you're outlining the trim, not just draping across the top. A bay window or double-wide garage door will run longer — measure those individually rather than using the average.

Step 3: Calculate Tree Coverage by Density

Tree lighting is the one area where "correct" is really a style choice. The formula is height × bulbs-per-foot, but bulbs-per-foot depends entirely on how full you want the tree to look:

DensityBulbs per Foot of Height6-ft Tree Example
Sparse (accent glow)~60360 bulbs

Dense (professional/showstopper)~150900 bulbs

If you're not sure which density you want, look at a decorated tree at a neighbor's house or a store display before you buy — it's a much cheaper way to calibrate your eye than returning strands after the fact.

Step 4: Convert Total Feet to Strands

Once you have a total linear-feet number across every area, divide by your strand's length: mini-light strands typically run about 33 ft (100 count at 4-inch spacing), while C9 strands typically run about 25 ft (25 count at 12-inch spacing). Round up — a partial strand still counts as a full one when you're buying.

Common Christmas Light Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating corners and peaks. A roofline total that skips the extra length used wrapping each corner almost always comes up one strand short.
  • Daisy-chaining too many incandescent strands. Voltage drop visibly dims the far end of a run past 3-5 connected strands — run a separate extension cord to the far side of a long roofline instead of one long chain.
  • Using indoor-rated lights outside. Confirm an outdoor (wet-location) rating before hanging anything outside; indoor strands aren't sealed against rain and snow.
  • Mixing incandescent and LED strands on one run. The different current draw can trip a breaker or damage the LED strand — keep the two types on separate circuits.
  • Skipping the pre-hang test. Plug in and check every strand on the ground first. Finding one dead bulb after it's clipped to a two-story roofline is a much worse afternoon than finding it at ground level.
Pro tip: Buy 10-15% more lights than your calculation shows. Extra strands become spare parts for burnt-out bulbs mid-season and give you slack to redo a section that looks uneven the first time you plug it in.

Frequently Asked Questions

How far apart should Christmas light bulbs be spaced?

Mini lights are typically spaced 4 inches apart, giving a dense, twinkly look. C9 and C7 bulbs are spaced about 12 inches apart for a bolder, more classic roofline look with fewer total bulbs.

Should I measure lights needed before or after buying a ladder?

Measure first. You can estimate roofline length from the ground using a measuring wheel, a tape along the foundation, or Google Maps satellite measuring tool, so you know your light quantity and budget before you ever need to get on a roof.

How much extra should I buy beyond my calculated total?

Buy 10-15% more than your calculated footage. Spare strands cover burnt-out bulbs mid-season, uneven first attempts at wrapping a tree, and small measurement errors on gables or bay windows.

Are permanent (all-season) Christmas lights worth it?

Permanent LED light channels installed under the roofline stay up year-round and can be controlled by app for any holiday or color scheme. They cost significantly more upfront (installed pricing, not a per-strand purchase) but eliminate the annual ladder work and storage of standard string lights.
