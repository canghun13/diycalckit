How Much Baseboard Do I Need? Room & Whole-House Guide

July 24, 2026 · DIYCalcKit

Baseboard is sold and installed by the linear foot, so the whole estimate comes down to one number: the perimeter of every room you're trimming, adjusted for doorways and waste. Get that number right and the board count and cost follow automatically.

Step 1: Measure the Perimeter, Not the Floor Area

Add up all four wall lengths in each room — this is your baseboard run before any adjustments. A common mistake is estimating from square footage instead; a 120 sq ft room could be a long narrow 8x15 or a near-square 11x11, and those two shapes need very different amounts of baseboard even though the floor area matches.

Step 2: Subtract for Doorways

Baseboard stops at the door casing rather than running across the opening, so subtract roughly 3 feet of baseboard for each doorway in the room. This is easy to forget when you're totaling up a whole house with a dozen door openings — it adds up to several extra boards if skipped.

Step 3: Add a Waste Allowance

Inside and outside corners require miter or coped cuts that use more material than a straight run. Budget 10% extra for a simple rectangular room, 15% as a solid default for most homes, and 20% if the room has lots of corners and angles or you're new to cutting trim.

Step 4: Add Door and Window Casing (If You're Replacing It)

OpeningTypical Casing Footage
Standard interior door (one side)~17 linear feet
Standard window~10 linear feet

A door needs casing on both sides of the wall if both rooms are getting new trim — double the per-door figure if you're finishing both faces of the opening.

Step 5: Decide on Crown Molding Separately

Crown molding runs the full ceiling perimeter with no door deduction, since it continues uninterrupted above every opening. Its compound miter cuts at each corner also waste more material than baseboard's simpler 45-degree cuts, so budget a few extra percent of waste on top of your standard allowance if you're adding crown to the same room.

Step 6: Convert to Boards

Trim stock typically comes in 8, 10, 12, and 16-foot lengths. Divide your total linear footage by your chosen board length and round up — a partial board still counts as a full purchase. Matching board length to your longest wall minimizes both seams and waste; a 12-ft wall with 12-ft stock needs no splice at all.

Common Baseboard & Trim Mistakes

  • Forgetting the door deduction. Baseboard doesn't run across doorway openings — subtract roughly 3 ft per door or you'll over-order.
  • Estimating from floor area instead of perimeter. Two rooms with the same square footage can need very different amounts of baseboard depending on shape.
  • Skipping the waste allowance. Corner cuts use more material than a straight run; 10-15% extra prevents a mid-project supply run.
  • Mixing profile heights between adjacent rooms. A 3" baseboard next to a 5" baseboard reads as an unfinished remodel from the doorway between them.
  • Folding base shoe into the baseboard total. Base shoe nails to the floor, not the baseboard, so calculate and order it separately.
Pro tip: Order slightly more than your calculated total and keep the leftover pieces. A future repair or a scratched section is a much easier fix with matching material already in the garage than a return trip to match a discontinued profile.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much baseboard do I need for a room?

Measure the room's perimeter (add up all four wall lengths), subtract about 3 feet for each doorway, then add 10-15% for waste, corners, and cuts. A typical 12x14 ft room needs about 50-56 linear feet of baseboard.

How much baseboard does a whole house need?

A typical 1,500 sq ft home needs roughly 400-500 linear feet of baseboard, depending on layout and number of rooms. Add 10-15% waste to get your purchase total, plus door and window casing if you're replacing that trim too.

How much door and window casing do I need?

Budget about 17 linear feet of casing per standard interior door (both side legs plus the top header) and about 10 linear feet per standard window. Wider or taller openings need proportionally more.

Does crown molding use more material than baseboard?

Crown molding typically uses slightly more linear footage than baseboard for the same room because it runs the full ceiling perimeter uninterrupted by doorways, and the compound miter cuts at each corner waste more material than baseboard's simpler cuts.
