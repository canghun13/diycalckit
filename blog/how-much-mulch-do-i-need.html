How Much Mulch Do I Need? Complete Guide for Homeowners

Updated May 2026 · 5 min read

Buying the right amount of mulch saves you a second trip to the store — and helps you avoid buying too much. The formula is simple: multiply your bed area by mulch depth, divide by 12, and divide by 27 to get cubic yards. Here's everything you need to know.

How Deep Should Mulch Be?

The ideal mulch depth is 2–4 inches. Shallower than 2 inches and weeds push through; deeper than 4 inches can suffocate plant roots and cause crown rot. For most garden beds, 3 inches is the sweet spot.

DepthBest ForNotes
2 inchesEstablished beds with dense plantingGood for moisture retention
3 inchesMost garden bedsBest weed suppression
4 inchesTree rings, pathwaysMaximum — don't go deeper

Bags vs. Bulk: Which Is Cheaper?

Bagged mulch from Home Depot or Lowe's typically runs $4–$7 per 2 cubic foot bag. Bulk mulch (by the cubic yard) from a landscape supplier costs $25–$45 per yard delivered.

One cubic yard = 13.5 bags of 2 cu ft mulch. At $5 per bag, that's $67.50 in bags vs. $35 in bulk for the same volume. Bulk is almost always cheaper if you need more than 3 yards. For small projects under 1 yard, bags are more practical.

How to Calculate Mulch Needed

The formula: (Length × Width × Depth in inches) ÷ 12 ÷ 27 = Cubic Yards

Then: Cubic Yards × 13.5 = Number of 2 cu ft bags

For example, a 10×4 ft bed at 3 inches deep needs: 10 × 4 × 3 ÷ 12 ÷ 27 = 0.37 cubic yards, or about 5 bags (plus 10% buffer = 6 bags).

Best Time to Buy Mulch

Mulch is cheapest in late fall and early winter when demand drops. Spring is peak season — prices at Home Depot and Lowe's are highest in April and May. If you can mulch in November and rake it back in spring, you'll save 20–30%.

Mulch Types and Their Best Uses

  • Shredded bark/wood chips — Most common, good for all beds, breaks down slowly
  • Pine straw (pine needles) — Great for acid-loving plants, lightweight, lasts well
  • Cedar mulch — Natural insect repellent, good for foundation plantings
  • Rubber mulch — Playgrounds and pathways, doesn't decompose
  • Straw — Vegetable gardens, decomposes quickly and adds nutrients
