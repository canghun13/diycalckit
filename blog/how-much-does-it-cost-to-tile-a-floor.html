How Much Does It Cost to Tile a Floor in 2026?

Updated May 2026 · 8 min read

Tile flooring costs between $7 and $25 per square foot installed in 2026, including materials and labor. For a standard 10×10 bathroom floor (100 sq ft), expect to pay $700–$2,500 total. For a larger kitchen or entryway at 200 sq ft, budget $1,400–$5,000. The final number depends heavily on tile type, pattern complexity, and local labor rates.

What Affects Tile Installation Cost?

Tile size and pattern significantly impact labor cost. Large-format tiles (18×18 or larger) require more careful leveling and take longer to set. Diagonal or herringbone patterns add 15–25% to labor because of additional cuts and layout time. A simple straight-set grid pattern is always the most affordable option.

Subfloor condition is a hidden cost driver that catches many homeowners off guard. If the existing subfloor is uneven, cracked, or needs a waterproofing membrane (required for all wet areas), expect to add $2–$5 per sq ft before any tile goes down. Older homes with wood subfloors often need cement board underlayment, adding another $1–$2 per sq ft.

Grout and setting materials add $1–$3 per sq ft. Epoxy grout costs $5–$8 per square foot more than standard cement grout but is completely stain-resistant and never needs sealing — worth it in kitchens and bathrooms. Standard grout is fine for low-traffic areas but should be sealed annually in wet areas.

Tile removal, if you're replacing existing tile, adds $2–$4 per sq ft in labor. Old tile can also reveal surprises underneath — cracked concrete, outdated vinyl, or moisture damage — all of which add to the total.

DIY vs. Hiring a Tile Setter

Tiling is a realistic DIY project for simple, rectangular rooms with straight walls and minimal cuts. Labor typically runs $4–$9 per sq ft, so a 100 sq ft bathroom floor saves $400–$900 by doing it yourself. The materials and tools (tile saw, trowels, spacers, grout float) cost about $100–$300 to rent or buy.

That said, a bad tile job is expensive to fix. Uneven tiles, poor grout lines, and improper waterproofing are the most common DIY mistakes — and correcting them means tearing out the entire floor and starting over. If this is your first tile project, start with a low-stakes space like a mudroom or laundry room before tackling a master bathroom.

Hire a professional for: any wet area (shower floors, bathroom floors near the toilet), natural stone that requires sealing and special adhesive, large-format tiles that require a perfectly flat substrate, and any floor with radiant heat underneath.

How to Calculate Tiles Needed

Divide your room's square footage by the tile size (in sq ft) and add 10–15% for waste. A 10×10 room needs about 111 tiles of 12×12 tiles at 10% waste. For diagonal patterns, increase waste to 15–20%.

Tips to Save on Tile Installation

  • Buy tile at the same time — dye lots vary between production runs; mixing batches causes visible color differences
  • Stick to in-stock tile — special-order tiles can add 2–4 weeks and 20–40% to material cost
  • Use a straight-set pattern — saves 15–25% in labor vs. diagonal or herringbone
  • Do demo yourself — pulling up old flooring before the installer arrives can save $200–$500
  • Get 3+ quotes — tile installation prices vary significantly by contractor and market

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to tile a floor? A professional can tile a standard 100 sq ft bathroom floor in one day, but the tile needs 24 hours to set before grouting, and grout needs another 24 hours to cure before getting wet. Plan for 3–4 days total before the floor is fully usable.

Do I need to seal tile floors? Glazed ceramic and porcelain tiles don't need sealing, but the grout does — especially in wet areas. Natural stone tiles (travertine, marble, slate) need to be sealed before grouting and annually after. Unsealed stone absorbs stains quickly.

Can tile be installed over existing tile? Yes, in most cases, as long as the existing tile is firmly adhered and the floor can handle the additional height and weight. This avoids the cost and mess of demo, but limits how close doors can close and can cause issues at transitions.
