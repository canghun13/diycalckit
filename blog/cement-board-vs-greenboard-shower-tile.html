Cement Board vs. Greenboard: What Goes Behind Tile in a Shower?

Updated July 2026 · 6 min read

You've torn out the old shower surround and you're staring at bare studs, ready to close the wall back up before tiling. This is the exact point where a lot of DIY bathroom projects go wrong — not the tiling itself, but what goes behind it. Greenboard and cement board look similar enough that it's easy to assume either one works. Only one of them is actually rated for the inside of a shower.

The Core Difference

Greenboard is ordinary gypsum drywall with a water-resistant paper facing — the gypsum core itself is still paper-wrapped, and that paper core will break down under sustained, direct water contact even though the facing sheds splashes and humidity fine. It's a moisture-resistant product, not a waterproof one.

Cement board (like HardieBacker or Durock) has a cement-based core with no paper to rot. It doesn't structurally break down when soaked, which is why it's the standard tile backer for showers, tub surrounds, and other direct-water zones — but it isn't waterproof by itself either; it still absorbs water and needs a separate waterproofing layer in a wet area.

When Cement Board Is Required

  • Inside the shower or tub enclosure itself. Any wall that gets direct water spray needs cement board (or an equivalent waterproof panel system), not greenboard — this is the standard most local codes and tile manufacturers reference.
  • Floor-to-ceiling tile in a wet zone. If tile runs from the shower floor up past the showerhead, the entire tiled area behind it should be cement board, not just the lower portion.
  • Under any tile that will see standing water or constant spray — tub decks, shower niches, and steam shower walls all fall into this category.

When Greenboard Is Still Fine

  • Bathroom walls outside the direct spray zone. A wall behind a vanity, toilet, or general bathroom wall with normal humidity but no direct water contact can still use greenboard.
  • Bathroom ceilings away from the shower. Some manufacturers still caution against greenboard on any ceiling due to sag risk over time — check the specific product's installation guidance.
  • Budget backing for a half-bath or powder room with no tub or shower at all, where moisture exposure is minimal.

Don't Forget the Waterproofing Layer

Neither material is actually waterproof by itself — cement board resists breaking down when wet, but it still absorbs moisture, so a shower assembly needs a separate waterproofing step: a sheet membrane, a liquid-applied membrane painted or rolled on, or a fully waterproof panel system (like a foam-core tile backer) that combines the backer board and the waterproofing into one product. Skipping this step is one of the most common causes of shower leaks and hidden mold, even when the backer board choice was correct.

Common Mistakes

  • Using greenboard inside the shower because "it's still water-resistant." Water-resistant is not the same as rated for direct, sustained water contact — this is the single most common mistake on this topic, and it's a code violation in most jurisdictions.
  • Assuming cement board alone is waterproof. Cement board without a waterproofing membrane will still let water reach the studs over time through the joints and screw holes.
  • Mixing backer types on the same wall without planning the transition. If cement board stops partway up a wall with greenboard above it, plan that transition line carefully — it should land above the tile line, not at a random height.
  • Not checking local code before assuming the cheaper option is allowed. Some jurisdictions have stricter requirements than the manufacturer minimum — always confirm before you close up the wall.

The Bottom Line

If tile is going anywhere inside a shower or tub enclosure, use cement board (or a waterproof panel system) plus a proper waterproofing membrane — greenboard is not rated for that job, regardless of how it looks or feels once installed. Save greenboard for bathroom walls and ceilings that stay damp but never see direct water spray.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use greenboard behind shower tile?

No, not in the shower or tub enclosure itself. Greenboard is only moisture-resistant, not waterproof, and most manufacturers and building codes restrict it to damp areas without direct water contact, such as a bathroom wall away from the tub or shower spray zone.

Do I need a waterproof membrane over cement board in a shower?

Yes. Cement board resists breaking down when wet, but it is not waterproof on its own — it still absorbs moisture. Most manufacturers and the TCNA Handbook call for a waterproofing membrane (sheet, liquid-applied, or a fully waterproof panel system) over or built into the assembly in a shower.

What is the difference between cement board and greenboard?

Greenboard is gypsum drywall with a water-resistant paper facing — it resists moisture but its gypsum core still breaks down under direct, sustained water contact. Cement board has a cement-based core with no paper facing to rot, so it holds up in direct-water zones like shower walls and tub surrounds.

Is greenboard ever acceptable in a bathroom?

Yes, on walls and ceilings in the bathroom that don't get direct water spray or standing moisture, such as the wall behind a vanity or a ceiling outside the shower area. It's not rated for inside a tub or shower enclosure.
