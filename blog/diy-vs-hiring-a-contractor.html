DIY vs. Hiring a Contractor: When to Do It Yourself

Updated May 2026 · 7 min read

DIY home improvement is tempting — you save money, learn new skills, and get the satisfaction of doing it yourself. But not every project is worth tackling alone. Choosing the wrong approach can cost more than hiring a pro from the start.

Projects That Are Great for DIY

These projects have low technical barriers, are forgiving of beginner mistakes, and save significant money when done yourself:

  • Painting walls and ceilings — The most DIY-friendly home project. Saves $300–$800 per room.
  • Installing flooring (LVP, laminate) — Click-lock systems are genuinely beginner-friendly. Saves $2–$5/sq ft in labor.
  • Landscaping and mulching — No permits, no licenses needed. Pure savings.
  • Replacing light fixtures and outlets — Easy if you're comfortable turning off the breaker. Saves $75–$150 per fixture.
  • Caulking, patching, and minor repairs — Always DIY. A $10 tube of caulk vs. a $100 service call.
  • Installing backsplash tile — More forgiving than floor tile. Small tiles and peel-and-stick options make this very DIY-friendly.

Projects That Almost Always Need a Pro

These involve safety risks, permits, or specialized skills where mistakes are costly:

  • Electrical panel work — Never DIY. Safety risk and code compliance issues.
  • Structural changes — Moving or removing walls requires engineering knowledge. Always hire.
  • HVAC installation — Refrigerant handling requires EPA certification. Always hire.
  • Roofing (full replacement) — Fall risk, warranty considerations, and complexity. Hire.
  • Major plumbing (new lines, sewer work) — One mistake can mean $5,000+ in water damage.
  • Gas line work — No exceptions. Always hire a licensed plumber or gas fitter.

The Middle Ground: Hybrid Approach

For many projects, the best approach is hybrid — do the prep and cleanup yourself, and hire a pro for the skilled work. This is especially effective for painting (you tape and patch, they paint), bathroom tile (demo yourself, hire for installation), and landscaping (you plant, hire for grading and drainage).

How to Calculate If DIY Is Worth It

Estimate the labor portion of a project quote (typically 40–60% of total cost). Compare it to the value of your time and the risk of a redo. If a contractor charges $2,000 for a job where $1,000 is labor, ask yourself: is 20+ hours of my time worth $1,000, and can I do it well enough the first time?

When DIY Goes Wrong: Real Costs

A bad DIY tile job that needs to be torn out and redone costs more than hiring a pro initially. Incorrect electrical wiring can void insurance coverage. Unpermitted structural work can create headaches at resale. Factor these risks into your decision.
