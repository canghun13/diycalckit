How to Calculate Square Footage

Multiply length × width for each rectangular room and add the totals together. For odd-shaped rooms, divide them into rectangles, calculate each section, and sum the results. For L-shaped rooms, split at the inside corner into two rectangles and add both areas.

Always measure at the widest points of each room. Don't subtract for furniture or built-ins — flooring and renovation materials run underneath or around these. Do measure closets separately; a standard reach-in adds 8–12 sq ft, a walk-in adds 25–60 sq ft.

What to Use Square Footage For

Knowing your room or home square footage is the starting point for most home improvement estimates. Flooring requires square footage plus a waste factor (typically 10% for tile, 5–8% for LVP and hardwood). Paint coverage is based on wall area — calculate that separately by multiplying perimeter by ceiling height. Renovation cost estimates (per sq ft pricing for kitchen remodels, additions, etc.) all start with accurate square footage.

For real estate purposes, finished square footage typically excludes garages, unfinished basements, and spaces with ceilings under 7 feet. When buying or selling, always verify the recorded square footage against your own measurements — discrepancies of 5–10% are not uncommon in older listings.

Pro tip: For flooring projects, measure each room separately and add closets individually. Apply the waste factor to the combined total, not room by room — this reduces over-ordering on small rooms.
