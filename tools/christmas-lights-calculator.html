How to Measure for Christmas Lights

Measure the roofline along the eaves — the horizontal edges where the roof overhangs the wall — not the sloped gable ends. Walk the perimeter with a tape measure or measuring wheel, or trace it on Google Maps satellite view and use the measure-distance tool for a rough estimate without a ladder.

Add about 10% to your roofline total for corner wraps, plug-in slack, and clip spacing loss. Each corner or peak typically eats up an extra 6-10 feet of usable light run because bulbs bunch up going around the turn.

For windows, measure the outer trim perimeter (roughly 18 ft for a standard window). For doors, use about 20 ft for the frame. For trees, the density you choose matters more than the exact formula — walk around a decorated tree at a neighbor's house or in a store display to gauge how full you want yours to look before you commit to "dense."

Common Christmas Light Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating corners and peaks. A roofline total that ignores the extra length used up wrapping each corner almost always comes up short by one full strand.
  • Daisy-chaining too many incandescent strands. Voltage drop makes the far end of the run visibly dimmer past 3-5 connected strands — run a separate extension cord to the far side of a long roofline instead.
  • Using indoor-rated lights outside. Always check for an outdoor (wet-location) rating; indoor strands aren't sealed against rain and snow and can short out or become a shock hazard.
  • Mixing old strand voltages with new LED strands. LEDs and incandescents shouldn't be connected end-to-end on the same run — their draw is too different and can trip a breaker or damage the LEDs.
  • Not testing every strand before hanging it. Finding one bad bulb after the lights are clipped to a two-story roofline is a much worse afternoon than finding it on the ground.
Pro tip: Buy 10-15% more lights than your calculation shows. Extra strands become spare parts for burnt-out bulbs mid-season and give you slack to redo a section that looks uneven the first time you plug in.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many feet of Christmas lights do I need for my house?

Start by measuring your roofline (the horizontal eaves, not the sloped edges), then add roughly 10% for corners and overlap. A typical single-story home needs 100-150 feet for the roofline alone; add more for windows, doors, and trees.

How many strands of lights do I need?

Divide your total linear feet by the strand length. Standard mini-light strands run about 33 feet (100 count at 4-inch spacing), while C9 bulb strands typically run 25 feet at 12-inch spacing. A 150 ft roofline needs about 5 mini-light strands or 6 C9 strands.

How many lights does a 6-foot Christmas tree need?

For medium coverage, plan on roughly 100 mini lights per vertical foot of tree — about 600 lights for a 6-foot tree. Dense, professional-looking coverage runs closer to 150 lights per foot (900 for a 6-foot tree), while sparse accent coverage can use as few as 60 per foot.

Mini lights or C9 bulbs — which uses fewer feet?

C9 bulbs need fewer total lights because they're spaced further apart (12 inches vs. 4 inches for minis), but mini lights create denser, twinklier coverage per foot. C9s are the classic bold roofline look; minis are better for wrapping trees, railings, and bushes.

Can I plug multiple strands of lights together?

Most incandescent strands can be connected end-to-end up to 3-5 strands (200-350 lights) before voltage drop dims the far end. LED strands can typically be connected 40-50+ strands because they draw far less current. Check the tag on your specific lights for the exact end-to-end limit.
