How Much Primer Does a Room Need?

A standard gallon of primer covers 350 square feet on a previously painted surface. For bare drywall or new construction, plan for 250 sq ft per gallon as the surface is more porous and absorbs more primer.

To calculate wall area: add up the perimeter of the room, multiply by ceiling height, then subtract 21 sq ft per door and 15 sq ft per window.

Do I Need Primer Before Painting?

Primer is essential when painting bare drywall, going from a dark to a light color, covering stains, or painting over glossy surfaces. For minor touch-ups on already-painted walls, you can often skip primer with a paint-and-primer combo product.

When Do You Actually Need Primer?

Not every paint job needs a separate primer coat. Modern paint-and-primer-in-one products are sufficient for most repaints over existing paint in good condition. Dedicated primer is necessary when: painting new drywall (paper-faced drywall must be primed or the paint will absorb unevenly), making dramatic color changes (dark to light or vice versa), covering water stains, smoke, or grease stains, painting over high-gloss surfaces, or painting over bare wood or metal.

Using the wrong primer for the surface is a common mistake. Water-based (latex) primer works for most drywall and interior walls. Oil-based (alkyd) primer is better for stain blocking, bare wood with tannins, and surfaces that bleed through. Shellac-based primer is the gold standard for blocking severe stains, odors, and knots in wood — nothing else performs as well on problem surfaces.

Pro tip: Always apply primer before painting bare drywall — skipping it leads to uneven color and extra coats of paint, costing more in the long run.
