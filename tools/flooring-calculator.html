How to Calculate Flooring for a Room

Multiply the room length by the width to get square footage. Add a waste factor of 10% for standard installations, or 15% for diagonal layouts. For multiple rooms, calculate each one separately and add the totals.

Most flooring is sold by the square foot or in boxes that cover a certain area. Always check the box coverage before buying.

Flooring Waste Factor Guide

  • 5% — Simple rectangular room, straight lay
  • 10% — Standard — most rooms
  • 15% — Diagonal install, L-shaped rooms, herringbone

How to Buy Flooring Without Over-Ordering

Calculate each room separately, then add the totals before applying your waste factor. Buying boxes to cover all rooms combined — rather than room by room — usually means fewer leftover boxes because you're working with a larger base number. Most flooring manufacturers recommend ordering from a single production run (same box batch number) to avoid subtle color variation between lots.

If your flooring comes in boxes that cover a fixed square footage (common for LVP and laminate), divide your total adjusted square footage by the box coverage to get the number of boxes. Always round up — never order half a box short. The cost of a return trip to buy one more box, plus the risk of a different lot number, isn't worth the $40–$60 savings.

Pro tip: Always buy 10% more than your calculated amount. Leftover flooring can be used for future repairs if a plank gets damaged.

Common Flooring Ordering Mistakes

  • Skipping the acclimation period. Hardwood and LVP need 48-72 hours in the room they'll be installed in before install, so the material can adjust to local humidity. Installing straight out of a cold delivery truck can cause gaps or buckling weeks later.
  • Using a flat 10% waste factor for every layout. A simple square room only needs 5-8% extra for plank flooring. A room with diagonal cuts, herringbone pattern, or lots of closets and jogs can need 15-20% — using the low number leaves you short mid-install.
  • Not checking subfloor flatness first. Rigid LVP and laminate require a subfloor flat within about 3/16" over 10 feet. Skipping this check is the most common cause of clicking, popping, or seam separation after install.
  • Ignoring plank direction for small rooms. Running planks the wrong direction (across the narrow dimension instead of the long one) can make a small room feel choppier and sometimes wastes more material at the cut ends.
  • Forgetting transition strips and thresholds in the budget. Every doorway between two different flooring types (or a change in subfloor height) typically needs a transition strip — an easy line item to leave off a DIY materials list.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate flooring for multiple rooms?

Calculate each room's area separately, subtracting closets or fixed obstacles as needed, add them together, then apply your waste percentage to the combined total.

What waste percentage should I use for flooring?

10% is standard for a rectangular room with a straight lay. Use 15% for diagonal layouts or irregularly shaped rooms.

Does flooring type change how much I need to order?

Waste percentage is similar across most hard flooring types, but always check whether your product is sold by box or plank, since box coverage varies by brand.

Do I need extra flooring for transitions between rooms?

Transition strips don't require extra material themselves, but if flooring must pattern-match across a doorway, factor in a small buffer for matching cuts.
