How to Calculate Mulch Needed

To calculate how much mulch you need, multiply the length and width of your garden bed to get the area in square feet. Then multiply by the depth in inches, divide by 12 to convert to feet, and divide by 27 to get cubic yards.

The formula is: (Length × Width × Depth in inches) ÷ 12 ÷ 27 = Cubic Yards

Most bags of mulch sold at Home Depot and Lowe's contain 2 cubic feet. One cubic yard equals 13.5 bags of 2 cu ft mulch. Always buy 10% extra to account for settling and uneven areas.

Recommended Mulch Depth

The ideal mulch depth is 2–4 inches. Going deeper than 4 inches can suffocate plant roots. For most garden beds, 3 inches is the sweet spot — enough to suppress weeds and retain moisture without harming plants.

Organic mulches (wood, bark, straw) break down over time and improve soil structure as they decompose — this is a feature, not a problem, but means you'll need to replenish annually. Inorganic mulches (rubber, gravel, landscape fabric) don't improve soil but last much longer. For vegetable gardens, organic mulch that can be turned into the soil at season end is almost always the better choice.

Pro tip: Measure each garden bed separately and add the totals. Don't forget to subtract any areas taken up by large plants or trees.

Common Mulching Mistakes to Avoid

  • "Mulch volcanoes" around trees — Piling mulch up against the trunk traps moisture against the bark, invites rot and insects, and can eventually girdle the tree. Keep mulch 2–3 inches away from any trunk or stem, in a flat donut shape rather than a cone.
  • Going deeper than 4 inches — More isn't better. Excess depth suffocates roots by blocking oxygen and water, and can encourage roots to grow up into the mulch layer instead of down into the soil.
  • Buying bags for a bulk-sized job — Once a bed needs more than about 3 cubic yards, bulk delivery from a landscape supplier is almost always cheaper per cubic yard than bagged mulch — often by half.
  • Skipping the 10% buffer — Garden beds are rarely perfect rectangles. Curved edges and plants eat into your usable area, so a bed that measures "enough" on paper often comes up short in practice.
  • Mulching straight over established weeds — Mulch suppresses new weed germination well, but it does little to weeds already rooted underneath. Pull or cut them back first, or the mulch just becomes a warm blanket for regrowth.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many bags of mulch do I need for a 10x10 garden bed?

A 10x10 ft bed at 2-inch depth = 100 sq ft × 0.167 ft = 16.7 cubic feet. Divide by 2 (for 2 cu ft bags) = 8.3 bags — order 9 bags. At 3 cu ft bags, you need 6 bags.

How deep should I apply mulch?

Apply 2–3 inches for established beds, 3–4 inches for new beds or heavy weed suppression. Never apply more than 4 inches — excessive depth prevents oxygen from reaching roots and causes stem rot.

How much does a yard of mulch cover?

One cubic yard covers 162 sq ft at 2-inch depth, 108 sq ft at 3-inch depth, or 81 sq ft at 4-inch depth. A cubic yard equals 27 cubic feet, or roughly 13–14 standard 2 cu ft bags.

Is it cheaper to buy mulch in bags or bulk?

Bulk mulch is almost always cheaper above 3 cubic yards. Bulk delivery typically runs $25–$45 per cubic yard delivered. Bags cost $4–$8 each — at 2 cu ft per bag, that's $54–$108 per cubic yard equivalent. The break-even point is usually around 3–4 cubic yards.

How often should I replace mulch?

Refresh mulch to 2–3 inches each spring — it typically settles and decomposes by 1–2 inches over the season. Replace mulch entirely every 3–4 years as it fully decomposes into the soil, which is actually beneficial for soil health.
